QUASARS

A quasar is a highly luminous, distant and energetic object that emits massive amounts of radiation across the entire electromagnetic spectrum. The term "quasar" is short for "quasi-stellar radio source", as these objects were initially identified as point-like sources of radio waves with no visible counterpart. Quasars are thought to be powered by supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies, which are surrounded by disks of gas and dust that are heated up and emit radiation as they are consumed by the black hole. This process generates a tremendous amount of energy, making quasars some of the most luminous objects in the universe. Quasars were first observed in the 1960s, and they have since been studied extensively using telescopes across the entire electromagnetic spectrum, from radio waves to gamma rays. They have provided important insights into the early universe and the formation and evolution of galaxies.
